## Summary
Indonesia's energy transition faces a financing problem disguised as policy paralysis — analysts propose a 500-megawatt coal retirement pilot funded by Japan's JBIC as a "proof of concept" to unlock concessional capital flows.

## Key Points
• **Financing Gap**: Early coal retirement is expensive due to long-term power purchase agreements with guaranteed revenues; concessional capital (cheaper, longer-tenor, risk-tolerant) is essential to bridge this gap
• **JBIC Role**: Japan Bank for International Cooperation has signaled support for Indonesia's decarbonization through partnerships with state utilities like PT SMI and PLN
• **Market Signal**: A concrete 500MW deal would establish a replicable template and signal to investors that coal retirement is "bankable" — addressing current skepticism
• **JETP Context**: Just Energy Transition Partnership analyses cite "policy distortions" and over-reliance on commercial debt rather than concessional capital as key barriers

## Portfolio Impact
🟡 **Neutral-Watch** — Article discusses coal transition financing but no immediate price impact expected.

**ITMG (Coal)**: Long-term structural headwind as Indonesia pursues net-zero 2060 target. However, near-term coal dominance continues. Monitor for any actual plant retirement announcements affecting demand forecasts.

**ADRO (Coal/Alamtri)**: Same sector dynamics as ITMG. Company has diversification into renewables via Alamtri — watch if they position as "transition partner" for early retirement deals.

**PGEO (Geothermal)**: 🟢 **Positive** — Geothermal benefits from transition credibility push. If JBIC facility materializes, expect more concessional capital to flow into geothermal expansion (PGEO's core business). Pertamina's geothermal arm well-positioned as state partner.
